McGill's Critical Media Lab

Researcher

At the Scrolling Societies Lab (within McGill’s Critical Media Lab), I served as a research assistant on a multimodal study examining how college students engage with TikTok’s seemingly infinite feeds. I helped design and implement an experimental research setting that brings participants and researchers together in collective feed-analysis sessions, combining affective computing, eye-tracking data, participant journaling, and free-associative image-work grounded in participants’ recorded TikTok feeds.

As the only member of the research team with a computer science background, I contributed technical expertise to the design of data collection workflows and the integration of computational methods with qualitative and phenomenological analysis. This interdisciplinary position allowed me to bridge technical measurement with critical theory, particularly in exploring how algorithmic systems shape attention, affect, and un/conscious experience during binge-scrolling.

In parallel with this work, I co-authored a post-modern theoretical piece emerging from the project, published in the Italian anarchist journal Lo Squaderno. This research ultimately advances a phenomenological psycho-politics of scrolling that moves beyond technophobic critique to understand social media feeds as affective technologies of presence.
