Philosophers often fancy themselves godlike— objective watchers of the world. I have always rejected this posture, pursued lines of inquiry rooted in my own situated experience: the class context in which I was raised, the conditions shaping my generation. While I remain attentive to the fact that my perspective is necessarily partial and biased, I do not see this as a disqualifying feature. On the contrary, I take it as the condition of possibility for meaningful philosophical work.

Over several years, I noticed my writing increasingly gravitate towards the digital. This led me to pursue a degree from McGill University in Computer Science and Philosophy. I wanted to not only think and write about technology, but participate in it. This reflects me as a person; I’ve never been one to stand by and watch. Throughout my undergraduate degree I’ve sought opportunities at the intersection of programming, digital media, and theoretical inquiry.

Looking ahead, I am particularly interested in developing personal projects that pair philosophical writing with tangible, technical components. For example, I am currently programming a dummy dating app to accompany my recent exploration of dating apps as the paradigmatic site of Marx’s commodity fetishism as applied to human relationships. Through such work, I aim to think critically with digital systems rather than merely about them.
